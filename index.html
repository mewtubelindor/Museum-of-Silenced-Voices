The Rose that Grew From Concrete

Tupac Shakur's Life and Legacy

Explore the influence of Tupac Shakur, a poet, activist, and West Coast rapper, on the American Dream. Growing up in gang communities and poverty, Tupac inspired others to make change by drawing from his own life story and dedicating himself to rapping about the truth. Listen to his most powerful tracks and symbolic lyrics about oppression, discrimination, and social issues.

Interview Archive

1994 • MTV Interview

“I’m not saying I’m gonna ... change the world, but I guarantee that I will spark the brain that will change the world”

In 1994, two year before his passing, Tupac Shakur sat down for an MTV interview in which he criticized other rappers and black artists for "leeching off the Ghetto", suggesting that many successful people were moving away from their difficult upbringings. In an inspirational message, he urged others to make change by understanding the importance of their words and actions as leaders.

Keep Ya Head Up

1993 • Strictly 4 My N.I.G.G.A.Z...

Listen to this feminist anthem written by 2Pac, one of the first songs in gangsta culture to reference pro-choice politics and rape culture. It influenced the next generation of black music, demonstrating Shakur’s dedication to rapping about the truth, society, and real problems.

“I think the shit that I say, no one else says. Who was writing about Black women before ‘Keep Ya Head Up?’ Now everybody got a song about Black women. Who was writing about that when I was writing about that? Who was writing about their own problems?”

The Rose That Grew From Concrete

1999 • Poetry Collection

Shakur wrote a collection of poetry between 1989 and 1991 that was posthumously released in 1999 by MTV Books. The collection's cover poem is a testament to the resilience of those in poverty and the ability to rise out of oppression. Listen to this artist's rendition of The Rose That Grew from Concrete.

“You see you wouldn't ask why the rose that grew from the concrete had damaged petals. On the contrary, we would all celebrate its tenacity. We would all love its will to reach the sun”

How does this metaphor for overcoming opression reflect Shakur's own life story?

Changes ft. Talent

1998 • Greatest Hits

One of the greatest rap songs of all time, and the only song to be posthumously nominated for Best Rap Solo Performance, Changes was recorded in 1992 and remixed featuring Talent, an R&B trio from Kansas City. The song highlights the uncomfortable realities of the world, seeking to explain why things are the way they are. Why do drug dealers sell to kids? Why is there violence in the streets? Tupac answers, "that's the way it is."

Trapped

1991 • 2Pacalypse Now

Shakur’s worldview emerges early in his career: he argues that Black men are pressured by peers, the law, and society’s refusal to help them, making violence a justified response. His belief in violence as a transformative power made him a controversial figure, but Shakur persisted in this cynical view of society.

They got me trapped, can barely walk the city streets

I'm telling you one day these suckers gotta fall
